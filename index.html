Why Venus?

A hellish wasteland

The surface of Venus is extremely uninhabitable. The atmosphere mainly consists of poisonous carbon dioxide. Temperatures at the equator average around 450°C, which is hot enough to melt lead, and the atmospheric pressure is 96 bar, or equivalent to 1km underwater on Earth. A thick cloud layer makes sunlight scarce and the clouds themselves are made of sulfuric acid.

Above the clouds

However, as NASA engineer Geoffrey A. Landis observed in his 2003 paper Colonization of Venus, 50km above the surface Venus is the most earthlike place in the Solar System besides the Earth itself.

  • Temperatures range from 0-75°C and pressure is close to 1 bar.
  • Since 50km is above the cloud layer, solar energy is abundant.
  • The atmosphere is still thick enough at this altitude to provide protection from cosmic rays equivalent to Earth's atmosphere at sea level.
  • Gravity on Venus is about 90% of Earth's, which could prevent bone loss and other health problems associated with microgravity.

On this page we will summarize the work of Landis and subsequent findings by NASA on the long term feasibility of exploring and settling Venus.

Pre-colonization

Studying Venus' runaway greenhouse effect may help us understand how to prevent the Earth from becoming like it.

Crewed exploration could begin with a design similar to Nasa's High Altitude Venus Operational Concept, or HAVOC for short. This would be a thirty-day mission aboard a blimp-like craft, testing the various systems and maneuvers required to maintain a habitat long-term. The craft would be solar-powered and coated in Teflon to protect it from sulfuric acid aerosol droplets.

If a more permanent presence is established in the atmosphere, it could be a more practical location from which to operate surface-exploring robots than one in orbit. In addition to the advantages discussed above, signals would take less time to travel between the habitat and robots on the surface, allowing near real-time monitoring and control.

Long-term settlement

Cities in the sky

Thanks to the relatively high density of its mostly carbon-dioxide atmosphere, a breathable air mixture of nitrogen and oxygen is a lifting gas on Venus. This means that the full volume of an aerostat can be used as a habitat. In fact, the lifting force of air on Venus is so great that a city-sized volume of it can lift a city. Such a city would float at about an altitude of 50km, where temperatures stay around 75°C. A seperate volume filled with a lighter gas like hydrogen could be used to lift habitats 5km higher, where temperatures average a pleasant 27°C (Wikipedia contributors, "Colonization of Venus"). In either case, air pressure inside such a habitat would be nearly equal to its surroundings, so ruptures would result in slow leaks rather than the explosive decompression that would occur in space or on Mars.

Sustaining a colony

Although free oxygen and water are virtually nonexistent on Venus, the materials needed to sustain a colony exist there in abundance in forms future colonists can unlock with sufficient energy.

  • Electrolysis of sulfuric acid and carbon dioxide can provide water and oxygen respectively.
  • Carbon dioxide and nitrogen captured from the atmosphere can also be used to grow plants which in turn would provide more oxygen and food.
  • Ore for construction and industry can be mined from the surface with robots and sent to the colony with aircraft or high-temperature fullerene tethers.

While the rotational period of Venus is 243 Earth days, much of its atmosphere rotates significantly faster than the planet itself. An atmospheric habitat untethered to the surface would thus travel around the planet in about four Earth days, creating a slow but still-reasonable day/night cycle.

Asteroid mining

In the long term, an outpost on Venus could be used to reach asteroids more quickly than launching missions from Earth, Mars, or even the asteroid belt itself. This is due to Venus' relatively fast orbital velocity, giving craft launched from Venus a greater starting velocity as well as increasing the number of chances a craft has to enter a transfer orbit to anywhere in the asteroid belt.
