The Case for Colonizing Venus

Why Venus?

A hellish wasteland

The surface of Venus is extremely uninhabitable. The atmosphere mainly consists of poisonous carbon dioxide. Temperatures at the equator average around 450째C, which is hot enough to melt lead, and the atmospheric pressure is 96 bar, or equivalent to 1km underwater on Earth. A thick cloud layer makes sunlight scarce and the clouds themselves are made of sulfuric acid.

Above the clouds

However, at an altitude roughly 50km above the surface, Venus is the most earthlike place in the Solar System besides the Earth itself. Temperatures there range from 0-75째C and pressure is close to 1 bar. Since 50km is above the cloud layer, solar energy is abundant, yet the atmosphere is still thick enough at this altitude to provide protection from cosmic rays equivalent to Earth's atmosphere at sea level. Finally, gravity on Venus is about 90% of Earth's, which could prevent bone loss and other health problems associated with microgravity.

Scientific exploration

Early exploration could begin with a design similar to Nasa's High Altitude Venus Operational Concept, or HAVOC for short. This would be a thirty-day crewed mission aboard a blimp-like craft, testing the various systems and maneuvers required to maintain a habitat long-term.

If a more permanent presence is established in the atmosphere, it could be a more practical location from which to operate surface-exploring robots than one in orbit. In addition to the advantages discussed above, signals would take less time to travel between the habitat and robots on the surface.

Habitat design

Cities in the sky

Thanks to the relatively high density of its mostly carbon-dioxide atmosphere, a breathable air mixture of nitrogen and oxygen is a lifting gas on Venus. This means that the full volume of an aerostat can be used as a habitat that will stay at an altitude of about 50km, where temperatures stay at a manageable 75째C. A seperate volume filled with a lighter gas like hydrogen can be used to lift habitats 5km higher, where temperatures average a pleasant 27째C.

Since the air pressure inside such a habitat would be about equal to its surroundings, ruptures would result in leaks rather than the explosive decompression that would occur in space or on Mars.

Exterior surfaces can be coated with teflon to protect them from sulfuric acid aerosol droplets.

Sustaining a colony

Although free oxygen and water are virtually nonexistent on Venus, the ingredients for life as we know it exist there in abundance in forms future colonists can unlock with sufficient energy. Electrolysis of sulfuric acid and carbon dioxide can provide water and oxygen respectively. Carbon dioxide and nitrogen can also be used to grow plants which in turn would provide more oxygen and food.

While the rotational period of Venus is 243 Earth days, much of its atmosphere rotates significantly faster near the equator. An atmospheric habitat untethered to the surface would thus travel around the planet in about four Earth days, creating a slow but still-reasonable day/night cycle.
